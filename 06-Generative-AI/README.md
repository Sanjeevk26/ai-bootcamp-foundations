# What is Generative AI?

Traditional AI focuses on:

- Analyzing data
- Classifying information
- Making predictions
- Recognizing patterns

Generative AI goes further.

It creates new content.

It can generate:

- Text
- Images
- Music
- Code
- Videos
- Game environments

Example:

Traditional AI:
Recognizes your face in a photo.

Generative AI:
Creates a new version of your face in a different setting — such as turning you into a gladiator or placing you inside a fantasy scene.

It does not just analyze.
It creates.

# The Technology Behind Generative AI

Generative AI builds on:

- Deep Learning
- Neural Networks
- Massive datasets

One important architecture historically used is:

## Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)

GANs work like two artists competing:

- One generates content.
- The other critiques it.
- They improve each other over time.

This adversarial training improves output quality.

Modern generative systems also rely heavily on large-scale language models and transformer architectures.

# Traditional AI vs Generative AI

## Traditional AI

- Classifies data
- Predicts outcomes
- Detects patterns
- Uses algorithms like regression and clustering
- Lower computational requirements

Examples:
- Spam detection
- Weather prediction
- Fraud detection
- Price prediction

---

## Generative AI

- Creates new content
- Uses deep neural networks
- Requires massive datasets
- Requires high computational power
- Generates text, images, audio, video

It is not just analytical.
It is creative.

# Why Is Generative AI So Important?

Generative AI is receiving global attention for several reasons:

1. Massive Economic Impact  
   It is projected to contribute trillions of dollars in value globally over the coming years.

2. Productivity Gains  
   Estimated productivity increases range between 15% and 40% in many knowledge-based tasks.

3. Acceleration of Creativity  
   It allows individuals and businesses to create content faster than ever before.

4. Early Stage Technology  
   We are still in the early stages of adoption, similar to the early days of the internet.

Generative AI is not just a trend.
It is a structural shift in how work is done.

# Business Use Case 1 – Personalized Learning

Imagine a tutoring app.

A student named Jane struggles with fractions and decimals.
She enjoys cooking and baking.

A Generative AI-powered tutoring system:

- Asks diagnostic questions
- Identifies knowledge gaps
- Customizes lessons
- Explains fractions using pizza slices
- Connects math concepts to baking measurements

Instead of generic lessons, the AI generates personalized explanations based on Jane’s interests.

Outcome:

- Improved understanding
- Higher engagement
- Increased confidence

This is content generation tailored to the learner.

# Common Generative AI Terms

You will frequently hear the following terms:

---

## Large Language Model (LLM)

A large neural network trained on massive text datasets.

Used for:
- Text generation
- Question answering
- Summarization
- Translation

---

## Foundation Model

A large pre-trained model that serves as a base.

Organizations can:
- Fine-tune it
- Customize it
- Build applications on top of it

It is the “foundation” upon which other AI systems are built.

---

## Multi-Modal Model

A model that processes multiple input types:

- Text
- Images
- Audio
- Video

Example:
Generate a blog article and a related image simultaneously.

---

## Prompt Engineering

The practice of designing effective instructions for AI systems.

A prompt tells the model:
- What to do
- How to respond
- What format to use
- What style to adopt

Better prompts produce better outputs.

---

## Parameters

Parameters are internal weights in neural networks.

Examples:
- 7 billion parameters
- 70 billion parameters
- 175 billion parameters

More parameters generally mean:
- Larger model capacity
- Greater complexity
- Higher computational requirements

---

## Fine-Tuning

Customizing a pre-trained model using domain-specific data.

Example:
- Training a medical model on medical data
- Training a legal model on legal documents

Fine-tuning adapts a general model to specialized use cases.

---

## Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

Large models are trained on static data.

They do not automatically know recent events.

RAG works by:

1. Retrieving current or company-specific data.
2. Feeding it into the model.
3. Generating responses using updated information.

It combines retrieval systems with generative models.

---

## Tokens

Tokens are units of text processed by language models.

They are not exactly equal to words.

Rough approximation:
- About 750 English words ≈ 1,000 tokens.

Tokens include:
- Words
- Sub-words
- Punctuation
- Spaces

Model usage and cost are often measured in tokens.
